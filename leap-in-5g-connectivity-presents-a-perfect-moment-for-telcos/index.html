Driving the
digital economy

ARTICLE 1

Leap in 5G connectivity presents a “perfect moment" for telcos

As digital technologies and innovations become embedded in the day-to-day lives and operations of consumers and enterprises respectively, the role of telcos has seen a significant transformation over the past decade.

Innovation with new technologies and their implementation are moot with poor network connectivity. Telcos play a critical role in efforts to leverage ICT investments and technologies—not only are they the “engines behind next generation networks,” they are also fundamental to ICT utilisation and driving the greater digital economy, says Audrey Plonk, head of the Digital Economy Policy Division at the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD).

In a survey of 500 tech executives, policy makers and institutional investors carried out by EI Studios, 74% of respondents say a strong digital ecosystem is needed to effectively leverage ICT. Implicit is the need for an ecosystem where access to basic and advanced connections is fundamental and will rely on carriers’ investments into physical and virtual infrastructure.

In a survey of 500 tech executives, policy makers and institutional investors carried out by EI Studios, 74% of respondents say a strong digital ecosystem is needed to effectively leverage ICT.

Enrique Blanco, global chief technology officer of Spanish telecommunications company Telefonica, says that while the first function of a telco is interconnectivity, this goes well beyond connectivity as we knew it two decades ago. “It is possible that you have digital processes and you build a digital approach, but without seamless connectivity and without low latency, the services you offer are not going to work,” he says, underlining the role telcos are playing in the digital economy today.

By taking fibre and 5G to even remote parts of Spain, Telefonica isn’t just allowing people to connect with one another, but providing them with improved access to a range of services and many necessities such as financial, health and retail services. Moreover, it supports the digital economy as even small businesses and institutions can become fully digitised.

“As telcos, we are spending a lot of money and time on this,” says Mr Blanco.

“You have to reach customers with fibre, and this means 5G or FTTH, and then you’ve got to develop improved customer experience.”

The provision of high-speed connectivity that is uninterrupted lays the foundation for other services such as automated capabilities, built on use cases that are based in algorithms and machine learning. “With all of the intelligence and AI capabilities, we are trying to build use cases for new services for the future,” Mr Blanco says.

Telcos function—to a great degree—as public utilities given that the connectivity they provide underpins almost all of the advances being witnessed in the digital economy, as well as digital industrialisation that is transforming the way enterprises conduct business, both from an operational as well as consumer offering standpoint.

All of this requires the development of ICT infrastructure and connectivity, and that, in turn, requires substantial investment. Mr Blanco, for example, says that year after year, telcos are managing substantially greater amounts of data capability and investing in R&D.

But there is a need for a shift in mindset.

As the telco’s role becomes more technology oriented as an enabler of a plethora of technological and digital services, Mr Blanco says there must be industry and regulator understanding that will enable or encourage telcos to continue investing in technologies.

”We need to, with the help of regulators and industry, change this way of thinking that the onus of investment lies on the telcos alone,” Mr Blanco says.

Given the growing understanding—and appreciation—of the importance of the digital economy in powering socio-economic prosperity, coupled with the big leaps being made with 5G network connectivity, Mr Blanco refers to the present as “the perfect moment” in trying to bring this change.

World-leading telcos striving to deliver faster and more stable connectivity will be gathered in the upcoming Mobile World Congress Shanghai (June 28-30) to share their innovations and progression, and redraw the possibilities of 5G.
